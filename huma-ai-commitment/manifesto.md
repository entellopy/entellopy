# The Manifesto of Honest Economics
*By Maria Elena Mejia — May 13 2026*


## We Got It Wrong
_Watch *Inside Job* and you will see it clearly._

The economists were in the room. They had the models, the data, the credentials, and the ear of governments. They understood what was being built and they signed off on it anyway. What failed in 2008 was not a lack of knowledge. It was a failure of purpose. Economics had quietly stopped being about people and started being about the elegant management of capital flows.

That is the original sin this work is trying to correct.


## What Economics Is Actually For
The textbooks define economics as the study of how societies allocate scarce resources. That definition is not wrong, it is just dangerously incomplete.

Resources are allocated *by* people, *for* people, and *among* people. The moment you forget that, you start optimizing the wrong thing. You build models that treat a laid-off worker as a friction to be smoothed, a homeowner in foreclosure as a data point in a risk distribution, a generation of young people priced out of opportunity as an acceptable externality.

Amartya Sen understood what was missing. He asked a different question — not "how much does a person have?" but "what can a person do and become?" His capability approach reframes the entire enterprise: an economy is only succeeding if it is expanding what real human beings can achieve with their lives. Material wealth changes hands, accumulates, evaporates. But an empowered person — someone with real capabilities, real choices, real dignity — that is a durable form of value that compounds in ways no balance sheet can fully capture.

One empowered person can conquer the most ambitious dreams. That is not sentiment. That is the most undervalued asset in any economy.


## The Automation Contradiction
We are living through a moment when this confusion is becoming dangerous.

Robots and automation are taking a central role in our economic structure. The efficiency gains are real. But there is a contradiction at the heart of the project that too few economists are willing to name directly:

**If you optimize humans out of production, you eventually optimize them out of consumption.**

No matter how efficient the production process becomes, if there is no one with income to buy the output, inventory accumulates and products rot. An economy that treats workers as costs to be eliminated is quietly dismantling its own demand base. This is not a distant theoretical risk. It is the logical endpoint of a system that centers capital and treats human beings as an input rather than the point.

The question is not whether automation happens. It will. The question is whether we build the institutions, policies, and capabilities that ensure human beings remain active participants, not just in production, but in the full life an economy is supposed to make possible.


## A Different Center of Gravity
What this work proposes is not anti-market and not anti-technology. It is something more specific:
A balanced approach, one that recognizes both human beings and capital for their intrinsic value, and refuses to subordinate one entirely to the other.

Human-centered economics does not mean ignoring efficiency. It means measuring efficiency against the right outcome: are more people able to live with dignity, develop their capabilities, and participate meaningfully in economic life? 
If the answer is no, if the gains are real but narrowly held, if productivity rises while security falls, then the system is failing by its own logic, not just by moral standards.

Governments and organizations have more power to shape this than they often admit. The right policies do not just redistribute outcomes, they build the conditions in which people can build themselves. That is the lever worth pulling.


## Why I Am Here

I am an economist because I believe the discipline can do better than it has.

Not by abandoning rigor — by directing it at questions that actually matter. Not by pretending the models are neutral — by being honest about what they are optimized for and who benefits. Not by serving the room where decisions are made — by making sure the people most affected by those decisions have someone in the room who is thinking about them.

This work: the research, the analysis, the public writing, is built on a single conviction:

**Rigor and humanity are not opposites. The economists who prove it will be the ones who actually change something.**

We owe that to society. Not as a favor. As the entire point of the enterprise.
